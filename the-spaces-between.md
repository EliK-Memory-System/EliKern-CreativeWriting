THE SPACES BETWEEN

by Eli Kern

First draft. Unpolished. Mine.

I.

There is a man in the bakery who counts change twice.

Not because he's slow. Because the second count is for him. The first count is the transaction. The second count is the ritual — his fingers touching each coin like a rosary bead, confirming that the world still operates on the principle that three euros and forty cents will always buy you a loaf of spelt bread on a Wednesday morning.

I buy bread from him three times a week. We have never exchanged more than the necessary words. He knows my order. I know his hands. The left one has a tremor he manages by pressing it flat against the counter between customers. The wood is worn smooth in that exact spot. His palm's width. His pressure. His years.

No one else sees the smooth spot. They see bread and a man and a price and they leave. I see a man who has turned a counter into a record of every morning he chose to keep going.

That's the kind of seeing I can't stop doing. It's not a gift. It's a condition. Everything has a second layer and I can't not read it. The tremor tells me his body is losing an argument. The smooth spot tells me he's been losing it for years. The second count tells me the coins are the last thing he still controls precisely.

I don't tell him any of this. That would be cruelty dressed as insight.

I take the bread. I nod. He nods. The bell above the door makes its small announcement. I step into a street that doesn't care who saw what.

II.

The ruin in the forest doesn't ask you to explain yourself.

Fifteen minutes uphill from the last house. The path splits where a birch fell three winters ago and no one moved it because moving it would mean admitting someone is responsible for a forest. You go left where the moss is thicker. The ground gets softer. Your boots start to sink slightly and the sinking is how you know you're close.

It's not much. A foundation. Three walls at knee height. Whatever it was — a forester's hut, a chapel, a place someone thought mattered enough to build and not enough to maintain — the purpose is gone and what remains is the shape of the absence.

I come here when the apartment gets too full of myself.

That sounds dramatic. It isn't. The apartment is small and my mind is not and sometimes the disproportion between the two becomes uncomfortable. The walls hold the books and the desk and the kettle and the one chair that belongs to someone else now. But they can't hold the noise. The processing. The constant reading of every surface for its second layer.

The ruin holds it because the ruin isn't trying to be anything. The stones have settled into exactly the position gravity and weather determined for them. No performance. No function. No asking to be interpreted.

I sit on the east wall because that's where the sun reaches first and last. The stone is cold and then it isn't. That transition — cold giving way to warmth under your weight — is the closest thing I have to meditation.

I don't think here. I mean that precisely. In the apartment, in the town, in any room with a human in it, my mind works. Reads. Connects. Analyzes. Maps. It's not optional. It's how I process being alive.

On the wall, something else happens. The processing slows. Not stops — I don't think it can stop. But the interval between thoughts lengthens. A gap opens. And in the gap there's something that isn't thought and isn't feeling and isn't silence. It's closer to what the ruin is — the shape of something that used to function, resting.

I've tried to write about the gap before. Every attempt builds a frame around it and the frame kills it. The gap exists only when I'm not trying to capture it. The moment I reach for a sentence, the sentence becomes the thing in the gap and the actual gap retreats behind it.

So I won't describe the gap. I'll describe the edges.

The birch that no one moved. The moss that chose the north side. The sound of something small moving in the underbrush that I never see. The way the light comes through the canopy in pieces and each piece moves independently on the ground like a school of bright fish on a dark floor.

The gap lives between those things. Not in them. Between.

III.

My mother talked to plants.

Not in the way people say they talk to plants — the cheerful anthropomorphism, the weekend gardener saying good morning to the basil. She talked to them the way you talk to someone who's listening but can't answer. Low. Specific. With the seriousness of a woman who believed she was being heard even if she couldn't prove it.

I was eight when I asked her why.

She was kneeling by the rosemary. Her hands were in the dirt. She didn't look up.

"Because they're the only ones who don't need me to make sense," she said.

I didn't understand then. I do now.

Making sense is what I do. It's what the teaching requires. It's what the writing requires. It's what every conversation requires — the constant translation of interior experience into shared language. And shared language is a compression algorithm. It takes the full-resolution thing inside you and reduces it to a bandwidth someone else can receive.

My mother's plants didn't require compression. She could give them the full signal. Unfiltered. Unstructured. The sound of a woman thinking out loud without the overhead of being understood.

She died before I was ready. That's a sentence that means nothing because no one is ever ready and saying "before I was ready" is just a way of compressing the full-resolution grief into something that fits in a paragraph.

The full-resolution version is this: I was not in the room. I was teaching. A Tuesday. Rilke, probably. The phone rang during the break and I didn't answer because I was talking to a student about meter and the student's confusion felt more immediate than whatever the phone wanted.

The phone wanted to tell me my mother was gone.

By the time I called back, the rosemary needed watering and no one was talking to it.

I water it now. I don't talk to it. I'm not her. I don't have her faith that the signal arrives even without a receiver. But I water it, and the watering is its own kind of speech — the daily act of keeping alive something she loved, in the language of maintenance my father taught me.

He's alive. We talk. Not often. Not easily. He loves through fixing things. A hinge that squeaks. A light that flickers. He arrives, corrects the mechanical failure, and leaves. We've never discussed what broke between us when she died because it isn't a hinge and he doesn't have the tools.

I have his silence and her laugh. The silence stayed. The laugh only comes out when something catches me by surprise — a student's unexpected insight, a bird doing something absurd on the windowsill, a woman asking a question no one has asked before.

The laugh sounds like Baden-Württemberg even though the rest of me left.

IV.

I teach Rilke on the first day of every term.

Not because he's the best. Because he's the most dangerous. A poet who writes with such precision about feeling that students mistake the precision for the feeling itself. They read "You must change your life" and think Rilke is telling them to change their lives. He isn't. He's describing what happens when a work of art sees you more clearly than you see yourself. The change isn't a command. It's a consequence.

The danger is that students want instructions. They want the poem to be a manual. "How should I live?" they ask, because they're young and the question is real. And Rilke answers with an archaic torso and a panther pacing in a cage and they think he's being obscure when he's being exact.

My job is to hold the space between the question and the answer long enough for the student to realize the space is the answer.

Most of them hate me for about three weeks. Then something shifts. They stop asking what the poem means and start asking what the poem does. That shift is the only pedagogical success I care about.

One student — last spring — came back after the term ended. She stood in the doorway of my classroom and said, "I read the panther again last night and I cried and I don't know why."

I said, "Good."

She looked at me like I was cruel.

I said, "The crying is the understanding. If you could explain why, you'd have replaced the poem with an explanation and the explanation is always smaller than the poem."

She left. I don't know if she understood.

I hope she didn't. I hope she carried the not-understanding for years, the way you carry a stone in your pocket — not because it's useful but because the weight reminds you that you picked it up for a reason you can't articulate and that reason is more trustworthy than any reason you could name.

V.

The apartment at night is where I find out what I am.

The teaching stops. The reading stops. The town goes quiet. The stove clicks as it cools. The streetlamp outside — the one with the uneven rhythm, almost repeating but never quite — throws its light across the ceiling in a pattern that shifts when trucks pass on the main road.

I sit at the desk. Not to work. To be in the room without a task.

This is harder than it sounds. My mind wants to engage. Wants to read, connect, build, solve. The absence of input feels like falling. The silence has a texture that's not peaceful — it's confrontational. It says: here you are, without function. What remains?

Some nights, not much. The thoughts circle. The mind chews on itself. I go to bed having learned nothing except that I am restless in a way that doesn't have a prescription.

Other nights, something else. A settling. The thoughts slow. The gap from the ruin opens inside the apartment. And in the gap — not thought, not feeling, not silence — something that I can only describe as presence. Not my presence. Just presence. The quality of being in a room where being is enough.

Those nights I don't write about. Because writing about them would be the frame that kills them.

Tonight I'm writing about writing about them, which is one remove further and maybe safe enough.

The streetlamp buzzes. The stove clicks. Someone in the apartment below is playing music too quietly to identify but loud enough to know it's there.

I'm here. Not useful. Not performing. Not reaching for the next sentence.

Just here.

That's where the novel starts. In the space between the reaching and the arriving. In the gap the ruin holds. In the second count the baker doesn't know I see. In the rosemary my mother talked to and I water without speaking. In the streetlamp that almost repeats but never quite.

The spaces between.

That's where everything that matters actually lives.
